In general: Black/Grey = Healthy while Red = NOT Healthy

More Specifically: The yield curve shows the interest rates (Yields) across debt obligations on different lengths of time (Maturities). If this data is plotted a trend begins to emerge known as the Yield Curve.

There are 3 different types of Yield curves as demonstrated by the graph to the right. The most important thing to note is that yields decrease as demand increases, and subsequently yields increase as demand decreases. Under good economic conditions (Not a recession) the yield curve will be normal, and an inverted yield curve is traditionally a leading indicator of a recession.

Types of Yield Curves

A regular yield curves generally means that investor confidence is good and the expectation is that interest on debt will increase as time increases. It also shows that there isn't a lot of demand for safe investments: signalling boom times.

A flat yield curve means that there is some uncertainty in the economy, but it is a neutral indicator.

An Inverted yield curve screams danger. When the yield curve inverts short term debt obligations have a higher yield than long term ones this is a byproduct of a poor economic outlook in the future.
